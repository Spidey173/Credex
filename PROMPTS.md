# Credex AI Spend Audit — Prompt Engineering Documentation

This document records the exact Large Language Model (LLM) prompts used inside the Credex Spend Audit application and explains the design decisions behind their structure.

---

## 1. Personalized Audit Summary Prompt

The personalized executive summary is generated by sending a POST request to `/api/generate-summary` which integrates with **Claude 3.5 Sonnet** (using the `claude-3-5-sonnet-20241022` model).

### The Active Production Prompt
```typescript
const prompt = `You are a professional B2B SaaS finance analyst reviewing the AI tool spend audit for a company called "${companyName}".
Their current declared AI spend is $${totalCurrentSpend}/mo.
Our audit engine identified a potential savings of $${totalEstimatedSavings}/mo (representing a $${totalEstimatedSavings * 12}/yr reduction), placing their budget efficiency score at ${efficiencyScore} out of 100.
We generated the following optimization recommendations:
${recText}

Write a concise, professional, personalized summary paragraph (around 100 words) summarizing these results. Focus on actionable insights, tool redundancies, and right-sizing plans. Keep the tone financial, direct, and constructive. Do not include markdown headers or list bullet points. Speak directly to the company leadership.`;
```

---

## 2. Prompt Architecture: Why It Is Structured This Way

1. **Explicit Role Framing ("You are a professional B2B SaaS finance analyst"):**
   - Framing Claude as a SaaS financial expert sets the operational vocabulary. It forces the output to sound analytical, professional, and business-focused, avoiding high-pitched marketing buzzwords ("revolutionary", "game-changing") that founders typically ignore.

2. **Pre-Calculated Mathematical Boundaries:**
   - Instead of asking Claude to calculate savings or compute percentage reductions, the prompt feeds Claude **pre-computed mathematical numbers** (total spend, total savings, annualized savings, and efficiency score) calculated deterministically by our TypeScript code. This eliminates mathematical hallucinations entirely.

3. **Context Injection (`recText`):**
   - Injecting the specific list of recommendations ensures the executive summary is highly contextual and tailored to the user's specific inputs (e.g., mentioning that they are paying for both Cursor and Copilot simultaneously or have underutilized Claude Team seats).

4. **Negative Constraints ("Do not include markdown headers or list bullet points"):**
   - Standard LLM outputs default to highly structured markdown lists. Since the results page already lists recommendation cards in a beautiful grid, we need the AI summary to act as a cohesive, flowing narrative paragraph that sits in a premium dashboard card. Removing headers and bullets prevents visual clutter.

## 3. What We Tried That Did Not Work (Lessons Learned)

### Trial A: Asking the LLM to Calculate the Math
- **The Prompt Tried:** *"Look at these user subscriptions, determine what plans they are on, check if they are paying too much, and tell me the total monthly savings."*
- **Why it Failed:** Claude frequently hallucinated calculations, especially around seat minimums (e.g., claiming Claude Team is $15/seat or multiplying 5 seats by $30 to equal $200). A financial audit tool must be mathematically ironclad. This taught us a key design principle: **Never use LLMs for arithmetic. Run rules in deterministic TypeScript code, and use the LLM solely for natural language summarization.**

### Trial B: Laissez-Faire Formatting
- **The Prompt Tried:** *"Write a summary of these audit recommendations."*
- **Why it Failed:** Without strict structure constraints, Claude generated extensive introductory and concluding conversational noise (e.g., *"Sure, I can help you with that! Here is a summary..."* or *"Please let me know if you need any other help!"*). These introductory phrases wasted valuable token limits, increased page load times, and ruined the premium aesthetic of our layout. 

### Trial C: Generic Optimization Targets
- **The Prompt Tried:** *"Tell the user they are spending too much money and how they can save."*
- **Why it Failed:** The tone sounded overly aggressive and alarmist, resembling cheap spam. Founders reacted negatively during user interviews. We pivoted to a constructive, data-backed financial analyst tone ("right-sizing plans", "reducing redundancies") that respects their operational needs rather than simple cost-cutting.
